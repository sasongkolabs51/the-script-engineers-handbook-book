## 2. Beat Script Builder Templates

These are ready‑to‑customize blueprints for each framework. Each beat includes its **emotional job**, **content requirement**, **mandatory field**, and a **prompt snippet** you can paste directly into your structural skeleton prompt. Adjust the number of beats or agitation layers based on your video length.

### 2.1 PAS Beat Script (Problem‑Agitation‑Solution)

| Beat | Emotional Job | Content Requirement | Mandatory Field | Prompt Snippet (paste into Layer 2 prompt) |
|------|---------------|---------------------|-----------------|--------------------------------------------|
| **1. Problem Identification** | Create instant recognition. The viewer must feel personally seen. | A specific, sensory‑rich scenario or statistic that names the problem in the viewer’s language. | One concrete detail (number, quote, personal anecdote fragment) that couldn’t apply to any other video. | “Describe a moment from my ideal viewer’s daily life that they instantly recognize as the problem. Use a specific sensory detail—time of day, a physical sensation, or a verbatim thought they’ve had.” |
| **2. Agitation Layer 1 – Immediate Frustrations** | Transform recognition into discomfort. The viewer feels the daily irritation. | Describe the immediate, tangible cost—wasted time, repeated mistakes, friction—using a concrete scenario. | A quantifiable cost or a repeated scenario. | “Explain how this problem manifests day‑to‑day. Avoid abstract language. Show the viewer losing something specific: twenty minutes every morning, a client’s trust, the ability to focus.” |
| **3. Agitation Layer 2 – Escalated Consequences** | Transform discomfort into genuine concern. The viewer grasps the long‑term stakes. | Expand the cost to larger life domains—career, relationships, self‑identity. Use a ‘what if this continues’ projection. | A forward‑looking statement that paints a vivid, personal future cost. | “Project forward six to twelve months. If this problem persists, what specifically will the viewer have lost? Write it as a ‘you will have…’ statement that feels both personal and painful.” |
| **4. Solution Introduction – The Pivot** | Shift from tension to hope, but earn it. The solution emerges as a revelation. | Describe the moment of insight or the counterintuitive reframe that changes everything. | A sentence beginning with “I realized…” or “The shift happened when…” | “Introduce the solution not as a list but as a single, powerful insight that redefines the problem. Use a first‑person moment of discovery if appropriate. The tone should be revelatory, not instructional.” |
| **5. Solution Expansion – How It Works** | Build credibility and clarity. The viewer understands the mechanism. | Break the solution into 2–3 clear steps with concrete examples. | At least one specific, repeatable action the viewer can take immediately, in simple language. | “Explain the solution step by step. Each step must include a concrete example from my niche. Use second‑person (‘you’) and present‑tense action language. Avoid jargon.” |
| **6. Empowered Future – The Payoff** | Create a sense of attainable transformation. The viewer feels capable and motivated. | Describe life after the solution, explicitly tying back to the original Problem from Beat 1. | A direct callback to the sensory detail or scenario from Beat 1, now transformed. | “Return to the scenario from Beat 1 and rewrite it as it looks after the solution. The same time of day, the same setting—but everything feels different. End with a statement of what the viewer now has that they didn’t before.” |

**Customisation:** For shorter videos, compress Agitation to one layer (merge beats 2 & 3). For deep‑dives, add a third Agitation layer before the pivot.

### 2.2 Hero’s Journey Beat Script (Video‑Condensed, Seven Beats)

| Beat | Emotional Job | Content Requirement | Mandatory Field | Prompt Snippet |
|------|---------------|---------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **1. Ordinary World** | Establish a relatable starting point. The viewer sees themselves in the story. | Describe the “before” state with concrete, sensory detail. | A specific, mundane moment (a morning routine, a familiar frustration). | “Paint a vivid picture of my (or the subject’s) ordinary world before the journey. Use sensory details that make it feel real and unremarkable.” |
| **2. Call to Adventure** | Introduce the disruption—an opportunity, challenge, or question that cannot be ignored. | Present the specific event or realisation that changes everything. | A clear, external trigger (a message, a failure, a chance encounter). | “Describe the moment the ordinary world was disrupted. Make it specific and unexpected. The viewer should feel a spark of curiosity or unease.” |
| **3. Refusal / Doubt** | Create tension; the journey must feel earned. | Show hesitation, fear, or the temptation to stay comfortable. | An internal monologue or a specific action that demonstrates resistance. | “I (or the subject) almost didn’t go. Describe the doubt, the excuses, the fear of failure—make it genuine, not clichéd.” |
| **4. Meeting the Mentor / Discovering the Insight** | Introduce the key knowledge that makes progress possible. | Reveal the person, book, quote, or epiphany that provides the crucial tool or perspective. | A direct statement of the insight: “What I learned was…” or “She told me…” | “Introduce the insight that broke through the doubt. It could be a mentor’s advice, a book, a conversation, or a sudden realisation. Make the insight feel earned and specific.” |
| **5. The Ordeal** | The emotional low point; the hardest struggle. | Describe the central crisis, failure, or moment of truth that tests everything. | A concrete scene of setback, loss, or painful realisation. | “This is the darkest moment. Describe a specific failure or crisis—not a vague ‘it was hard,’ but a scene with sensory details. What was lost? What almost broke?” |
| **6. The Reward / Transformation** | The breakthrough; the new capability or perspective gained through the ordeal. | Show the shift—what was learned, how the character changed, the tangible or intangible reward. | A clear before‑and‑after contrast, anchored in a specific outcome. | “Reveal the transformation. Show the new understanding, skill, or perspective that emerged from the ordeal. Contrast it directly with the Ordinary World.” |
| **7. Return with the Elixir** | Bring the lesson back to the viewer’s world. Make it universally actionable. | Translate the personal transformation into a teachable lesson or tool for the audience. | A direct application to the viewer’s life, phrased as “you can…” or “this means for you…” | “Now, bring the lesson home. Explain what the viewer can take from this journey and apply to their own life. Connect back to the ordinary world from Beat 1, showing how it could be transformed.” |

### 2.3 Hook‑Story‑Offer Beat Script (Four Beats)

| Beat | Emotional Job | Content Requirement | Mandatory Field | Prompt Snippet |
|------|---------------|---------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **1. Hook** | Stop the scroll; create a compelling promise. | Use a hook archetype from Chapter 5 (Curiosity Gap, Specific Promise, etc.) plus a retention mechanism. | Must include a specific, concrete detail that anchors the curiosity. | *Use your chosen Hook‑Loop prompt from Chapter 5.* |
| **2. Story** | Build emotional connection through a specific, relatable narrative. | Tell a personal or case‑study story that illustrates the problem and the discovery. | A real (or realistic) person, a clear timeline (e.g., “in three months”), a before‑and‑after contrast. | “Tell a short, powerful story that proves the promise of the hook. Give the main character a name (even if it’s me). Show the struggle, the turning point, and the result. Keep it vivid and specific.” |
| **3. Insight / Principle** | Extract the universal lesson. The viewer thinks, “That makes sense for me too.” | Distill the story into one clear, repeatable principle or framework. | A sentence that starts with “The principle is…” or “What this teaches is…” | “From the story, pull out the single core insight that applies to anyone. Make it a memorable statement—something a viewer could repeat to a friend.” |
| **4. Offer / CTA** | Convert emotional trust into action, naturally. | Present the specific action, product, or next step as the logical extension of the insight. | A clear, low‑friction call to action (subscribe, download, buy, reflect) that ties back to the hook. | “Frame the offer as the natural next step. It should feel like a service to the viewer, not a pitch. Use language like: ‘If you’re ready to [desired outcome], then [the offer] is how we do it.’” |
