## 1. The Six Hook Archetypes Reference Card

**Purpose:** A one‑page cheat sheet you can keep open while prompting. Each archetype includes its psychological principle and the exact structural instruction to paste into your prompt.

| Archetype | What It Does | Psychological Principle | Prompt Instruction Snippet |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Curiosity Gap** | Presents an intriguing, incomplete piece of information that the brain urgently wants to close. | Zeigarnik effect: incomplete tasks or knowledge create mental tension. | “Open with a specific, unexpected fact or number that contradicts a common belief in my niche. Immediately state that the explanation will come later, but do not reveal it yet. Make the viewer feel they are missing something crucial.” |
| **Contrarian Claim** | Directly challenges a widely accepted belief or best practice, triggering defensive attention. | Cognitive dissonance: the brain prioritizes information that conflicts with existing beliefs. | “Open with a blunt, confident statement that rejects a widely accepted best practice, popular tool, or common opinion in my niche. Make it sound like a hard‑won insight, not a hot take. Avoid softening phrases like ‘I think’ or ‘maybe.’ The claim should feel slightly risky but genuine.” |
| **Relatable Pain** | Describes a specific, often unspoken frustration that makes the viewer feel deeply understood. | Mirroring and identification: feeling seen creates instant trust and emotional bonding. | “Open with a detailed, almost uncomfortably specific description of a frustrating situation my ideal viewer has lived through. Use sensory details and precise language that makes the viewer think, ‘That’s exactly me.’ Do not name the solution yet; sit in the pain for a moment.” |
| **Specific Promise** | States a concrete, measurable outcome the viewer will gain, making the viewing investment feel worthwhile. | Outcome expectancy: clear, high‑value rewards increase motivation to continue. | “Open with a direct statement of exactly what the viewer will be able to do, understand, or avoid after watching. The promise must be specific and measurable—not ‘improve your productivity’ but ‘cut your weekly planning time from three hours to forty‑five minutes.’ Anchor it with a number or concrete capability.” |
| **Identity Challenge** | Names a positive identity the viewer holds, then suggests a hidden behavior is undermining it. | Self‑consistency: people are driven to resolve contradictions between their self‑image and their actions. | “Open by naming a positive identity my target viewer holds (e.g., ‘you’re the kind of person who values deep focus’) and immediately suggest that a routine habit is silently working against that identity. Create a gap between who they think they are and what they’re actually doing.” |
| **Open Loop** | Starts a story, scenario, or thought experiment and cuts off at the peak of tension. | Narrative closure need: the brain craves resolution, making an unfinished story intrusive until completed. | “Open with the beginning of a specific personal story, a ‘what would you do if’ scenario, or a step‑by‑step process—and stop abruptly at the moment of highest tension or uncertainty. Do not provide any resolution. Signal that the resolution will arrive later in the video.” |
