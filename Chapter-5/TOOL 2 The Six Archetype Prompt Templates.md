### Archetype 1: The Curiosity Gap

```
Write a YouTube video hook (first 2–3 sentences, max 40 words) for
the following:

TOPIC: [your specific topic]
AUDIENCE: [who's watching]
THE SURPRISING FACT: [a specific number, result, or finding that
contradicts what the audience assumes — e.g., "the $2,000 laptop
benchmarked 12% SLOWER than the $900 one in sustained rendering"]

INSTRUCTIONS:
- Open with the surprising fact stated plainly. No buildup. No
  "did you know." Just the fact, dropped like a stone.
- The fact must be SPECIFIC: a number, a date, a name, a
  measurable result. Not "surprisingly" or "interestingly."
- Immediately signal that the explanation is coming BUT do not
  reveal it. Create the gap. Make the viewer feel they are
  missing something crucial.
- End the hook mid-thought or with a forward-pointing phrase:
  "And the reason has nothing to do with [obvious answer]."
- Tone: [your voice — e.g., "matter-of-fact, slightly amused,
  like sharing a secret over coffee"]
- Do NOT use: "Have you ever wondered," "In this video,"
  "Let me tell you about," rhetorical questions.

Generate THREE distinct variations. Vary the opening structure:
- Variation A: Lead with the number/fact directly.
- Variation B: Lead with the moment of discovery ("I was running
  the benchmark when...").
- Variation C: Lead with the contradiction ("Everyone says X.
  My data says otherwise.").
```

### Archetype 2: The Contrarian Claim

```
Write a YouTube video hook (first 2–3 sentences, max 40 words) for
the following:

TOPIC: [your specific topic]
AUDIENCE: [who's watching]
THE CONTRARIAN CLAIM: [a position that directly challenges prevailing
wisdom in your niche — e.g., "the best productivity system is the
one you abandon after two weeks"]
WHY I BELIEVE THIS: [your earned reason — e.g., "after testing
31 systems in 2 years, the ones that 'stuck' made me less
productive, not more"]

INSTRUCTIONS:
- Open with the claim stated bluntly. No softening. No "I think"
  or "maybe" or "some people might say." State it like a fact
  you've earned through experience.
- The claim should feel slightly risky — like you're saying
  something that might get pushback. That tension is the hook.
- Immediately ground it: signal that this isn't a hot take but
  a hard-won insight. One phrase that implies experience:
  "After [X time/effort], I'm convinced..."
- Do NOT explain the reasoning yet. The claim stands alone.
  The explanation is the video.
- Tone: [your voice — e.g., "calm, confident, slightly
  provocative but not aggressive"]
- Do NOT use: "Unpopular opinion," "Hot take," "I know this
  is controversial," "Don't @ me."

Generate THREE variations:
- Variation A: State the claim in one blunt sentence. Then one
  sentence grounding it in experience.
- Variation B: State what "everyone" believes, then pivot with
  "They're wrong." Then the claim.
- Variation C: Open with the consequence of the common belief,
  then reveal your contrarian position as the fix.
```

### Archetype 3: Relatable Pain

```
Write a YouTube video hook (first 3–4 sentences, max 50 words) for
the following:

TOPIC: [your specific topic]
AUDIENCE: [who's watching]
THE SPECIFIC PAIN: [an uncomfortably precise frustration your
viewer has lived — e.g., "downloading a productivity app,
color-coding your entire week, feeling amazing for exactly
nine days, then opening the app on day ten and feeling
nothing but mild disgust"]
SENSORY DETAILS: [what they see, hear, feel in that moment —
e.g., "the little notification badge you keep swiping away,
the app icon you can't bring yourself to delete"]

INSTRUCTIONS:
- Open IN the pain. Drop the viewer into the specific moment.
  No preamble. No "Have you ever." Just the scene.
- Use sensory, precise language. Not "it's frustrating" but
  the exact texture of the frustration. The cold coffee. The
  1:47 a.m. screen glare. The specific app name.
- Make the viewer think "that's EXACTLY me" within the first
  sentence. The recognition IS the hook.
- Do NOT name the solution. Do NOT pivot to "but here's the
  fix." Sit in the pain for the full hook. Let them feel seen.
- End with a micro-escalation: hint that the pain is bigger
  or more common than they realized. "And the worst part?
  It's not a discipline problem."
- Tone: [your voice — e.g., "warm, slightly weary, like
  confessing to a friend who's been through the same thing"]

Generate THREE variations:
- Variation A: Second-person ("You're sitting there...").
  Drop the viewer into the scene.
- Variation B: First-person ("I was sitting there...").
  Share your own version of the pain.
- Variation C: Present-tense narration of the universal
  moment. ("Day nine. The app is still there. The
  color-coding is still perfect. And you feel... nothing.")
```

### Archetype 4: The Specific Promise

```
Write a YouTube video hook (first 2–3 sentences, max 40 words) for
the following:

TOPIC: [your specific topic]
AUDIENCE: [who's watching]
THE SPECIFIC OUTCOME: [a concrete, measurable result the viewer
will gain — e.g., "cut your weekly meal prep from 4 hours to
75 minutes" NOT "save time in the kitchen"]
THE PROOF ANCHOR: [a number, timeframe, or credential that makes
the promise credible — e.g., "I've done this every Sunday for
14 weeks"]

INSTRUCTIONS:
- Open with the promise stated directly and specifically.
  Include a NUMBER. Not "improve" but "cut from X to Y."
  Not "learn" but "be able to [specific capability] by [time]."
- Anchor the promise with proof: "I've [done X] for [Y time]"
  or "In the last [Z] videos/tests/days..."
- Make the investment feel small relative to the reward:
  "It takes [small effort] and gives you [large outcome]."
- Do NOT be salesy. Do NOT use exclamation points. State the
  promise like a calm fact, not a late-night infomercial.
- Tone: [your voice — e.g., "confident, understated, like
  sharing a tip you'd give a colleague"]
- Do NOT use: "By the end of this video you'll," "I'm going
  to teach you," "Get ready to," "Buckle up."

Generate THREE variations:
- Variation A: Promise first, proof second.
- Variation B: Proof first ("After 14 weeks of..."), promise second.
- Variation C: Contrast the old way vs. the new outcome.
  ("I used to spend 4 hours. Now it's 75 minutes. Same meals.
  Same quality. Here's the difference.")
```

### Archetype 5: The Identity Challenge

```
Write a YouTube video hook (first 3–4 sentences, max 50 words) for
the following:

TOPIC: [your specific topic]
AUDIENCE: [who's watching]
THE POSITIVE IDENTITY: [what the viewer believes about themselves
— e.g., "you're someone who values deep, focused work"]
THE UNDERMINING BEHAVIOR: [a common habit that contradicts that
identity — e.g., "you check Slack every 7 minutes and call
it 'staying responsive'"]
THE GAP: [the tension between identity and behavior — e.g.,
"you can't be a deep worker and a notification addict
simultaneously"]

INSTRUCTIONS:
- Open by NAMING the viewer's positive identity. Make them
  feel proud for one second: "You're the kind of person who..."
- Then immediately introduce the undermining behavior. Not
  as an accusation — as an observation. "And you also [behavior]."
- Let the contradiction sit. Don't resolve it. The discomfort
  of the gap IS the hook. The viewer needs to keep watching
  to resolve the tension.
- End with a gentle escalation: "And that gap? It's costing
  you more than [obvious cost]. It's costing you [deeper cost]."
- Tone: [your voice — e.g., "compassionate but direct. Not
  scolding. More like a friend saying 'hey, have you noticed
  this?'"]
- Do NOT use: "You're doing it wrong," "Stop doing X,"
  aggressive or shaming language. The challenge is gentle.
  The viewer should feel curious, not attacked.

Generate THREE variations:
- Variation A: Name identity → name behavior → let gap sit.
- Variation B: Describe the behavior first, then reveal it
  contradicts the identity they hold.
- Variation C: "You probably think of yourself as [identity].
  I did too. Then I tracked [behavior] for a week."
```

### Archetype 6: The Open Loop

```
Write a YouTube video hook (first 3–4 sentences, max 50 words) for
the following:

TOPIC: [your specific topic]
AUDIENCE: [who's watching]
THE STORY/SCENARIO: [a specific personal story, hypothetical, or
process that has a natural cliffhanger — e.g., "I was 40 minutes
into a client call when I realized the file I'd sent was the
wrong version"]
THE CLIFFHANGER MOMENT: [the exact point where you stop — the
moment of highest tension or uncertainty — e.g., "and the client
said four words that changed how I think about trust forever"]

INSTRUCTIONS:
- Open IN the story. Mid-action. No "Let me tell you about
  the time..." Just drop the viewer into the scene.
- Build quickly to the cliffhanger moment. 2–3 sentences max.
- STOP at the moment of highest tension. Do NOT resolve.
  Do NOT hint at the resolution. Just... stop.
- Signal (subtly) that the resolution is coming: "What happened
  next..." or "I'll tell you what those four words were in
  about three minutes. But first, you need context."
- The story must be SPECIFIC: a time, a place, a sensory
  detail. Not "something happened at work." But "Tuesday,
  2:15 p.m., the fluorescent light buzzing above my monitor."
- Tone: [your voice — e.g., "conversational, slightly
  breathless, like recounting something that still surprises you"]
- Do NOT use: "Story time," "So this one time," "You won't
  believe what happened."

Generate THREE variations:
- Variation A: Drop in mid-action. Build to cliffhanger. Stop.
- Variation B: Start with the aftermath ("I haven't been the
  same since Tuesday"), then flash back to the scene.
- Variation C: Start with the decision point ("I had two
  options. I picked the wrong one.").
```
